Overview

  • Increased frequency and severity of extreme weather events such as hurricanes, floods, and droughts
  • Changes in precipitation patterns and water availability

Our Atmosphere

The effects of global change on the atmosphere are complex and interconnected, and can have significant impacts on the Earth's climate, ecosystems, and human societies.

  • Increase in greenhouse gases:
    One of the most significant effects of global change on the atmosphere is the increase in greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, leading to a rise in global temperatures and changes in climate patterns.
  • Ozone depletion:
    Global change can also lead to the depletion of the ozone layer, which protects the Earth from harmful UV radiation. This depletion is primarily caused by the release of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and other ozone-depleting substances (ODS) into the atmosphere.
  • Changes in weather patterns:
    As global temperatures rise, weather patterns are likely to become more extreme and unpredictable. This can result in more frequent and severe heat waves, droughts, floods, hurricanes, and other extreme weather events.
  • Acidification of the atmosphere:
    Increased carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere can also lead to acidification, which can harm plants, animals, and ecosystems. Acid rain, for example, can damage forests, lakes, and other natural resources.
  • Changes in air quality:
    Global change can also lead to changes in air quality, as rising temperatures and changing weather patterns can increase the concentration of pollutants in the air. This can have negative impacts on human health, particularly for vulnerable populations such as children, the elderly, and people with respiratory or cardiovascular diseases.

Our Animals

Global change is having a profound impact on animal species, and it is crucial that we take steps to mitigate its effects and protect these important members of our planet's biodiversity.

  • Changes in distribution and range:
    As temperatures and weather patterns change, many species are forced to adapt by moving to new areas that can support their survival. This can lead to changes in the distribution and range of species, as well as alterations in migration patterns and breeding habits.
  • Habitat loss and fragmentation:
    Habitat destruction and fragmentation, often caused by human activities such as deforestation and urbanization, can have devastating effects on animal populations. It can lead to a loss of food and shelter, as well as the isolation of populations, making it difficult for individuals to find mates and maintain genetic diversity.
  • Changes in behavior and physiology:
    Animals are highly sensitive to changes in their environment, and global change can affect their behavior and physiology in various ways. For example, rising temperatures can alter breeding cycles and hibernation patterns, while pollution and other stressors can cause changes in immune function and reproductive success.
  • Species interactions:
    Global change can also affect the interactions between different animal species. For example, changes in the timing of plant growth and flowering can impact the availability of food for herbivores, which in turn can affect the populations of predators that rely on them.
  • Extinction risk:
    Ultimately, global change is increasing the risk of extinction for many animal species. The combination of habitat loss, changing environmental conditions, and other stressors can make it difficult for populations to survive and thrive, leading to declines and, in some cases, eventual extinction.

Our Oceans

Global change is having significant and far-reaching effects on the oceans, with potential consequences for human societies as well as marine ecosystems.

  • Ocean warming:
    The ocean absorbs much of the heat that is trapped by greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, which is causing the temperature of the ocean to rise. This warming can disrupt ocean ecosystems, including coral reefs, and affect the distribution of marine species.
  • Ocean Acidification:
    As carbon dioxide (CO2) is absorbed into the ocean, it reacts with seawater to form carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of the ocean. This change in chemistry can harm the ability of marine organisms such as corals and shellfish to build their shells and skeletons.
  • Sea level rise:
    AAs land ice melts due to warming temperatures, the resulting water flows into the ocean, causing sea levels to rise. This can lead to coastal flooding, erosion, and saltwater intrusion into freshwater resources.
  • Changes in ocean currents:
    Changes in temperature and salinity can disrupt ocean currents, which play a critical role in the transport of heat, nutrients, and oxygen around the world. This disruption can have cascading effects on marine ecosystems, including changes in the distribution of species and alterations to ocean productivity.
  • Marine biodiversity loss:
    As ocean temperatures, chemistry, and currents change, many marine species are being forced to adapt or move to new locations. This can result in shifts in the distribution and abundance of marine species, and even lead to extinction of some species.
